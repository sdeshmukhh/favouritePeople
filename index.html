Shri Premanand Maharaj

Premanand Govind Sharan Ji Maharaj, born as Aniruddh Kumar Pandey on March 30, 1969, in Akhri village near Kanpur, Uttar Pradesh, is a revered Indian Hindu guru and spiritual teacher associated with the Radhavallabh Sampradaya. From a young age, Maharaj Ji displayed a deep inclination towards spirituality. At just 13, he left his home to embrace a life of renunciation, taking vows of celibacy and becoming a sannyasi. Initially named Anand Swaroop Brahmachari, he later received the title Swami Anandashram after accepting the Mahavakya, marking his full commitment to ascetic life. aharaj Ji spent much of his early spiritual journey on the ghats of the Ganga in Varanasi, living a life of complete detachment and intense devotion. He practiced severe austerities, including fasting and enduring harsh weather, and remained immersed in meditation and spiritual practices. His spiritual quest eventually led him to Vrindavan, where a saint persuaded him to witness the Raas Leela, a pivotal moment in his journey. There, he was initiated into the Radhavallabh Sampradaya by receiving the "Sharanagati Mantra" and later the "Nij Mantra" from his Sadgurudev, Pujya Shri Hit Gaurangi Sharanji Maharaj, also known as Bade Guruji. This initiation introduced him to the profound spiritual states of Sahachari Bhava and Nitya Vihar Rasa, marking his entry among the Rasik saints—those deeply absorbed in the love of Radha-Krishna. Premanand Ji Maharaj is widely respected for his teachings on devotion, renunciation, and the path of selfless love for the divine. Despite facing severe health challenges in recent years, he continues to inspire countless followers with his discourses and unwavering commitment to spiritual ideals.

Dr. A.P.J Abdul Kalam

Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam, whose full name was Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, stands as one of India’s most revered scientists and leaders. Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, into a humble family, he overcame early financial hardships through determination and hard work. Kalam’s academic journey began at St. Joseph’s College, Tiruchirappalli, where he studied Physics, followed by Aeronautical Engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology.Kalam’s career began at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) in 1958, and he later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). At ISRO, he played a pivotal role in developing India’s first Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-3), which successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in 1980, marking a major milestone in India’s space program. His leadership in missile technology, particularly with the Agni and Prithvi missiles, earned him the title “Missile Man of India”.He specialized in aeronautical engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology and began his career at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) in 1958. Later, at the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), he was the project director for India's first indigenous Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III), which successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in 1980, marking India's entry into the exclusive "Space Club". Kalam played a pivotal role in developing the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) and, after moving back to DRDO, led the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), resulting in the creation of the Agni and Prithvi missiles.

Neerja Bhanot

Neerja Bhanot was a 22-year-old Indian flight purser who became an international symbol of courage during the hijacking of Pan Am Flight 73 on September 5, 1986. The flight, en route from Mumbai to New York with stops in Karachi and Frankfurt, was seized by four armed terrorists from the Abu Nidal Organization while on the ground at Karachi’s Jinnah International Airport. Neerja, the senior-most cabin crew member, quickly alerted the cockpit crew, enabling them to escape and thus preventing the hijackers from forcing the plane to take off—a move that likely saved hundreds of lives. Throughout the harrowing 17-hour ordeal, Neerja displayed remarkable composure and bravery. She hid the passports of American passengers to protect them from being targeted and offered constant reassurance to those onboard. When the hijackers began firing and setting off explosives, Neerja took charge of the evacuation, opening emergency exits and guiding passengers to safety. In her final act, she shielded three children from gunfire, sacrificing her own life to save theirs. Her selfless actions resulted in the survival of 360 out of 379 people on board. Neerja was posthumously awarded the Ashoka Chakra, India’s highest peacetime gallantry award, becoming its youngest and first female recipient. She also received commendations from the United States and Pakistan, including the Tamgha-e-Pakistan for her humanitarian efforts. Neerja Bhanot’s legacy endures as a powerful example of heroism, inspiring generations through her story, which was also depicted in the acclaimed 2016 film "Neerja".

Manoj Kumar Sharma

Manoj Kumar Sharma is an inspiring Indian Police Service (IPS) officer whose journey from failure to success has touched millions. Born in a small village in Morena district, Madhya Pradesh, he grew up in poverty and struggled academically—failing in Class 12 and passing only in his second attempt with just one subject cleared. Despite these setbacks, he never gave up on his dream of becoming an officer. To support himself while preparing for the UPSC exam, Manoj worked as a tempo driver, cleaned toilets, walked dogs, and spent long hours studying in a library. His turning point came when he began preparing seriously under the guidance of Vikas Divyakirti in Delhi. After failing the exam three times, he finally cracked it on his fourth attempt in 2005, securing All India Rank 121 and joining the IPS. Today, he serves as an Inspector General in CISF at Mumbai Airport and is respected for his dedication, honesty, and strong leadership. His life story was captured in the bestselling book Twelfth Fail by Anurag Pathak and was later adapted into the 2023 film 12th Fail, starring Vikrant Massey. The movie was a major success, and Manoj chose not to take any money for it, saying his story belonged to the people. His journey is a powerful example of how determination and hard work can overcome even the toughest obstacles.
